# Style Guide

This guide keeps the course coherent across contributors and languages.

## Voice

- Friendly, direct, and practical.
- Didactic, but not academic-heavy.
- Motivating, without hype replacing explanation.

## Writing principles

- Prefer cause-effect language over ornament.
- End important ideas with a reproducible action.
- Use heuristics, not absolutes, for music perception.
- Avoid repetitive disclaimer endings unless they add real clarity.

## Good vs avoid

- Prefer: "This interval often feels darker in context."
- Avoid: "3 semitones = dark."

- Prefer: "Try this in 20 seconds and compare A/B."
- Avoid: "Understand this concept deeply before moving on."

- Prefer ranges and listening criteria in mix guidance.
- Avoid fixed percentages as universal recipes.

## OP-1 Field terminology

Use consistently:

- `OP-1 Field`
- `Tape`
- `LFO`
- `Synth`
- `Mixer`
- `Input`
- `Resampling`
- `Shift`

## Interactive block labels

Use only:

- Spanish:
  - `pieza interactiva` (must include a real widget)
  - `ejercicio guiado opcional` (no real widget required)
- English:
  - `Interactive module` (must include a real widget)
  - `Optional guided exercise` (no real widget required)

Avoid deprecated labels such as:

- `dynamic piece`
- `optional dynamic part`

## Device claims

- Any physical shortcut or behavior claim should be:
  - verified in official docs, or
  - framed as a practical approximation.

## Translation quality

- Keep meaning, not literal calques.
- Keep module structure and ids unchanged.
- Keep interface terms aligned with device language.
